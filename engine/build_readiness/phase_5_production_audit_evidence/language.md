### Finding E-3

   edge_type = pedagogical_prerequisite
SEP-ANCHORED REASONING: SEP entries on "Presupposition" and "Implicature" present presupposition as one of the central pragmatic phenomena that fall outside the strictly truth-conditional content of an utterance — alongside conventional implicature, conversational implicature, and the speech-act force layer Austin/Searle theorized. Whether presupposition strictly *requires* speech-act theory as a prerequisite is contested in the literature: the Strawson 1950 presuppositional alternative to Russell's theory of descriptions predates Austin's 1955 lectures by five years and developed largely independently; Stalnaker's common-ground formalization frames presupposition in pragmatic terms (the conversational common ground is itself a quasi-speech-act-theoretic notion, but the formalization can stand without explicit Austin/Searle apparatus); the formal-pragmatics tradition (Heim, Kamp, Beaver) treats presupposition triggers as a lexical/compositional phenomenon with a discourse-update semantics, where the speech-act layer is one consumer of presupposition data rather than its prerequisite. ### Finding E-5
EDGE: causal_theory_of_reference [domain=language] → rigid_designator [domain=language]
   edge_type = pedagogical_prerequisite
SEP-ANCHORED REASONING: SEP entries on "Rigid Designators" and "Reference" present rigid designation as Kripke's technical term — introduced in Naming and Necessity (1972) as a formal apparatus for stating the modal argument against descriptivism — that became the central tool of contemporary modal semantics. Rigid designators ("Aristotle", "water") pick out the same object in every possible world in which that object exists; non-rigid designators ("the teacher of Alexander", "the colorless liquid in lakes and rivers") vary across worlds. The migration's edge runs causal_theory_of_reference → rigid_designator, implying causal-theoretic reference is a prerequisite for rigidity. Strictly the relationship is more delicate: rigid designation is a *property* an expression can have, definable independent of any specific theory of how reference is fixed (a rigid designator is one whose extension is constant across worlds; this can be checked semantically without committing to whether descriptions, causal chains, or some hybrid mechanism fix reference for any particular expression). Kripke's argumentative use of rigidity as a weapon against descriptivism does presuppose a competing view (the causal theory) that the rigid-designation property can be paired with — and pedagogically Kripke's treatment of rigidity is interwoven with his causal-theoretic story. So on the development-of-the-Kripkean-package reading, causal_theory_of_reference → rigid_designator is defensible: the rigid-designator notion as Kripke deployed it functions argumentatively only when paired with the causal-theoretic story about how rigid reference is fixed. But the rigid/non-rigid distinction as a pure semantic notion does not strictly require the causal theory; modal semantics adopted rigid designation independently of taking on the causal-theoretic apparatus wholesale. Edge supportable on the development-as-Kripke-presented-it reading; would be more accurate as proper_name → rigid_designator (proper names are the canonical rigid designators, and the rigid/non-rigid contrast is most easily introduced via the proper-name-vs-definite-description case that does not require the full causal-theoretic apparatus).
